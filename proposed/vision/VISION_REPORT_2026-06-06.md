# Vanguard1 Atmosphere & Lighting — Unified Vision Report

## Executive Summary

Across every camera angle — from global overview to the close-zoom Strait of Hormuz — the same fundamental failure repeats: **the map has no light, no atmosphere, and no volumetric presence.** The color palette, UI chrome, and data architecture are solid foundations, but the scene is rendered with what amounts to ambient-only illumination on flat textures, producing a result that reads as a styled 2D map rather than a holographic planetary projection. The single fastest path from "dark-mode web app" to "living tactical intelligence display" is a three-layer lighting stack: atmospheric rim glow, post-processing bloom, and emissive vessel points — in that order.

## Cross-View Patterns

**1. Zero atmospheric depth is universal.** Every view — polar, equatorial, oblique, close, far — exhibits the same hard transition from terrain texture to void. There is no limb glow, no horizon haze, no Fresnel rim, no scattering of any kind. This is the single most consistent and damaging gap. The globe does not read as a sphere suspended in space from any angle.

**2. The ocean is the largest dead zone.** At every zoom level, the ocean (which occupies 60-80% of screen real estate) contributes nothing atmospherically. It is a static dark texture. Multiple views independently identified this as wasted potential — the North Atlantic mid-zoom, the Hormuz close-up, the Arctic top-down, and the global overview all call out the ocean as inert. No specular response, no animated structure, no depth-responsive luminance.

**3. Vessel markers are static and undifferentiated.** Across views where vessels are visible, they appear as flat, same-brightness dots with no emission, no pulsation, no type differentiation, and no temporal encoding. They compete visually with text labels rather than dominating as primary data objects. Multiple analyses flag this as a critical failure of the "living ecosystem" goal.

**4. Landmasses are silhouettes, not surfaces.** From the Hormuz close-up (where Iran is a dark void) to the oblique dawn view (where Africa has flat color mapping but no directional shading), terrain consistently fails to communicate three-dimensionality. There is no rim lighting, no elevation-responsive shading, no emissive edge treatment at coastlines.

**5. Post-processing is absent.** No bloom pass exists. This means even the elements that are already emissive-coded (the cyan contour lines at Hormuz, the teal port clusters at Rotterdam) don't bleed light into their surroundings. Bloom is referenced in every single view analysis as a foundational missing layer. Its absence is the primary reason the scene reads as "rendered graphics" rather than "projected light."

**6. Performance is a gating concern.** The Arctic view reports 1 FPS. Any atmospheric enhancement must be profiled against the existing rendering budget. The proposals below are ordered with GPU cost in mind — the first three are shader-side additions with negligible per-frame cost; the later ones require more careful budgeting.

### Proposal 1: Atmospheric Rim Glow Shell (Fresnel Limb Lighting)

**Impact:** A thin, luminous cyan-blue halo traces the visible edge of the Earth where it meets space. At oblique angles (the dawn view), it thickens dramatically into a visible atmospheric band with warm-to-cool color gradation. At top-down views (Arctic, global), it manifests as a subtle radial brightness increase at the periphery of the visible globe. Landmass coastlines pick up a soft phosphorescent edge. The globe instantly transforms from "textured disc" to "planetary object suspended in a holographic projection field." Every view — every single one — benefits from this change. It is the foundational volumetric cue that every subsequent lighting effect compounds upon.

**Technique:**
- Create a slightly oversized sphere mesh (1.015x–1.03x globe radius) with `side: THREE.BackSide`, `transparent: true`, `depthWrite: false`, `blending: THREE.AdditiveBlending`.
- Custom `ShaderMaterial` with a Fresnel-driven fragment shader:
```glsl
uniform vec3 uAtmosphereColor; // vec3(0.1, 0.6, 1.0)
uniform vec3 uSunDirection;
uniform float uIntensity; // ~0.6

varying vec3 vNormal;
varying vec3 vViewDir;

void main() {
    float rim = 1.0 - max(dot(normalize(vViewDir), vNormal), 0.0);
    float atmosphere = pow(rim, 3.0) * uIntensity;
    
    // Warm shift on sun-facing side for dawn/dusk views
    float sunFacing = dot(vNormal, uSunDirection) * 0.5 + 0.5;
    vec3 color = mix(uAtmosphereColor, vec3(0.9, 0.6, 0.3), sunFacing * 0.3);
    
    gl_FragColor = vec4(color, atmosphere * smoothstep(0.0, 0.3, rim));
}
```
- Optional second shell at 1.05x radius, lower intensity (0.2), for outer atmospheric feather.
- For 2D/flat-map fallback: a radial gradient `ShaderPass` in `EffectComposer` that darkens edges and adds a blue-shifted peripheral glow.

**Affected Files:**
- `src/scene/AtmosphereManager.js` — **NEW** — owns the atmosphere shell mesh, shader, and sun-direction uniform updates.
- `src/scene/SceneSetup.js` (or equivalent globe initialization) — add atmosphere shell to scene graph after globe mesh.
- `src/rendering/RenderPipeline.js` (or wherever the render loop lives) — ensure atmosphere shell renders in correct pass order (after globe, before post-processing).

**Complexity:** LOW — One mesh, one shader, ~60 lines of code. The tuning iteration is the real cost, not the implementation.

**Holographic Vision:** This is the single most important holographic signifier. Real holograms and sci-fi command displays universally feature rim-lit, atmospherically-wrapped globes because the limb glow is what tells the human visual system "this object is volumetric and made of light." Without it, nothing else reads as holographic. With it, everything else compounds.

### Proposal 2: Post-Processing Bloom Pass

**Impact:** Every emissive element in the scene — the bathymetric contour lines at Hormuz, port cluster markers, UI panel borders, vessel icons, the atmospheric rim from Proposal 1, and future coastline glow — begins to bleed light into surrounding pixels. Bright elements stop looking *drawn* and start looking *projected*. The contour lines (which are already one of the strongest visual elements) suddenly feel like they're made of coherent light. The atmospheric shell gains a soft outer feather for free. The entire scene shifts from "rendered graphics on a screen" to "light projected into space." This is the universal amplifier that makes every other lighting proposal work harder.

**Technique:**
- Implement `EffectComposer` pipeline with `RenderPass` → `UnrealBloomPass` → output.
- Bloom parameters tuned conservatively:
  - `strength`: 0.35–0.5
  - `radius`: 0.6–0.8
  - `threshold`: 0.65–0.75 (critical: only the brightest elements should catch bloom — terrain and ocean base colors must fall below threshold)
- Optional: selective bloom via a separate emissive-only render target (render scene twice — once with only emissive materials contributing — and bloom only that target). This prevents terrain or ocean textures from blooming unintentionally but costs an extra render pass.
- Optional holographic overlay: a final `ShaderPass` adding extremely subtle scanlines (3-5% opacity, slow vertical scroll) and faint chromatic aberration at screen edges:
```glsl
uniform float uTime;
void main() {
    vec2 uv = vUv;
    // Scanline
    float scan = sin(gl_FragCoord.y * 1.5 + uTime * 0.5) * 0.025 + 0.975;
    // Chromatic aberration at edges
    float dist = length(uv - 0.5) * 2.0;
    float aberration = dist * dist * 0.003;
    vec3 color;
    color.r = texture2D(tDiffuse, uv + vec2(aberration, 0.0)).r;
    color.g = texture2D(tDiffuse, uv).g;
    color.b = texture2D(tDiffuse, uv - vec2(aberration, 0.0)).b;
    gl_FragColor = vec4(color * scan, 1.0);
}
```

**Affected Files:**
- `src/rendering/PostProcessingManager.js` — **NEW** — owns the `EffectComposer`, `RenderPass`, `UnrealBloomPass`, and any custom `ShaderPass` instances. Exposes methods to adjust bloom intensity dynamically (e.g., reduce during high-density views for readability).
- `src/rendering/RenderPipeline.js` — replace direct `renderer.render(scene, camera)` call with `composer.render()`.
- `package.json` / imports — ensure `three/examples/jsm/postprocessing/*` dependencies are available.

**Complexity:** LOW-MEDIUM — `UnrealBloomPass` is well-documented and ships with Three.js examples. The `EffectComposer` setup is ~30 lines. Scanlines and chromatic aberration are another ~20. The complexity is in threshold tuning: too low and the ocean blooms into mud; too high and nothing catches. Selective bloom (dual render target) adds MEDIUM complexity but significantly improves control.

**Holographic Vision:** Bloom is literally the visual phenomenon of light scattering through a projection medium. It is the definitive holographic effect. Without bloom, emissive elements look like colored pixels. With bloom, they look like sources of light. This single post-processing pass transforms every luminous element in the scene from graphical to photonic.

### Proposal 3: Vessel Point Emission with Animated Pulse Halos

**Impact:** Each vessel dot transforms from a static, undifferentiated sprite into a living point of light. A bright core (white-hot center, hue-shifted by vessel type — cyan for cargo, amber for tanker, green for fishing, red-orange for dark/AIS-off) sits at the center of a soft radial falloff halo. Each vessel pulses with a slow sinusoidal breathing (0.3–0.5 Hz, phase-randomized so they don't synchronize into a Christmas tree). Dark vessels pulse distinctly — slower, wider, ominously red, with a larger dim halo that reads as a warning beacon even in peripheral vision. When a vessel's last AIS ping ages beyond a threshold, its halo contracts and dims, encoding data staleness into luminance. Fresh AIS pings trigger a brief expanding ring — a sonar-ping ripple that expands and fades over 1.5–2 seconds — creating the sense of live data arriving in real time. Combined with Proposal 2's bloom pass, these emissive points bleed light into their surroundings, creating the signature "sensor display" look where every tracked object is a glowing presence rather than a database row.

**Technique:**
- Refactor vessel rendering from individual meshes/sprites to a single `THREE.Points` object (or `THREE.InstancedMesh` for more control) with a custom `ShaderMaterial`.
- Per-vessel attributes in `BufferGeometry`:
  - `aPosition` (vec3) — world position
  - `aType` (float) — vessel type index, used to look up color from a uniform array
  - `aPhaseOffset` (float) — random per-vessel, prevents synchronized pulsing
  - `aTimeSinceLastPing` (float) — updated each frame from data layer, drives halo contraction and sonar ring
  - `aAlertLevel` (float) — 0.0 normal, 1.0 dark/alert, drives red shift and wider halo
- Vertex shader: `gl_PointSize` scaled by zoom level and alert level.
- Fragment shader:
```glsl
uniform float uTime;
uniform vec3 uTypeColors[8]; // color palette per vessel type

varying float vType;
varying float vPhase;
varying float vPingAge;
varying float vAlert;

void main() {
    vec2 center = gl_PointCoord - 0.5;
    float dist = length(center) * 2.0;
    
    // Core + halo
    float core = exp(-dist * dist * 20.0);
    float halo = exp(-dist * dist * 3.0) * 0.4;
    
    // Pulse
    float pulse = sin(uTime * 2.5 + vPhase) * 0.15 + 0.85;
    
    // Alert treatment (dark vessels)
    float alertPulse = sin(uTime * 1.2 + vPhase) * 0.25 + 0.75;
    float activePulse = mix(pulse, alertPulse, vAlert);
    
    // Sonar ring on fresh ping
    float ringRadius = clamp(vPingAge * 2.0, 0.0, 1.0);
    float ring = smoothstep(0.03, 0.0, abs(dist - ringRadius)) * (1.0 - ringRadius);
    
    // Staleness: contract and dim
    float freshness = 1.0 - smoothstep(0.0, 3600.0, vPingAge);
    
    // Color
    int typeIdx = int(vType);
    vec3 baseColor = uTypeColors[typeIdx];
    vec3 alertColor = vec3(1.0, 0.3, 0.1);
    vec3 color = mix(baseColor, alertColor, vAlert);
    
    float alpha = (core + halo + ring) * activePulse * (0.3 + freshness * 0.7);
    
    if (alpha < 0.01) discard;
    gl_FragColor = vec4(color * (core * 2.0 + halo + ring), alpha);
}
```
- The `UnrealBloomPass` from Proposal 2 automatically catches these high-luminance fragments, providing the ambient glow for free.
- Update `aTimeSinceLastPing` each frame (or each data tick) to drive sonar rings and staleness dimming.

**Affected Files:**
- `src/vessels/VesselRenderer.js` (or equivalent) — **MAJOR REFACTOR** — replace per-vessel mesh/sprite creation with a single `Points` or `InstancedMesh` approach. This is the core change.
- `src/vessels/VesselBufferManager.js` — **NEW** — manages the `BufferGeometry` attributes, handles adding/removing vessels, and updates dynamic attributes (`aTimeSinceLastPing`) each frame.
- `src/shaders/vesselPoint.vert.glsl` / `src/shaders/vesselPoint.frag.glsl` — **NEW** — the shader pair above.
- `src/data/VesselDataStore.js` (or equivalent) — needs to expose vessel type and last-ping timestamp in a format the buffer manager can consume efficiently.

**Complexity:** MEDIUM — The shader is straightforward. The main engineering cost is the refactor from individual objects to instanced/batched rendering, and plumbing per-vessel data attributes into the buffer geometry. If vessels are already rendered as `Points`, this is much simpler. If they're individual `Mesh` objects, this is a meaningful architectural change — but one that also delivers significant performance improvement (one draw call instead of thousands).

**Holographic Vision:** Vessels are the primary data objects — the reason this map exists. If the vessels don't feel alive, the ecosystem doesn't feel alive. Pulsing, type-differentiated, bloom-caught points of light with sonar-ring arrival animations are the canonical "living sensor display" effect. This is what makes an operator look at the screen and think "I'm watching the world breathe" rather than "I'm querying a database."

### Proposal 4: Ocean Depth-Responsive Luminance with Animated Grid Underlay

**Impact:** The ocean — currently 60-80% of screen area contributing nothing — transforms into a living data surface. In the deep Atlantic trenches, a faint cool blue-green bioluminescence pulses from below. On continental shelves, warmer teal gradients communicate shallowing. Beneath it all, an extremely subtle hexagonal or rectilinear grid drifts slowly — like a holographic coordinate mesh projected just below the surface. The grid lines are nearly subliminal (opacity 0.04–0.08), faintly cyan, shifting with slow animated distortion as if seen through water or the wobble of a holographic projector. At grid intersections, nodes faintly brighten. In areas of high vessel density, the grid subtly intensifies, as if the display's resolution increases where attention is needed. At the Hormuz close-zoom, the bathymetric contour lines (already strong) gain a luminous substrate that makes the depth data feel embedded in a living medium rather than drawn on a dead surface. The overall effect: the void becomes structured, the ocean becomes intelligent, and every square pixel of screen real estate contributes to the holographic atmosphere.

**Technique:**
- Ocean surface `ShaderMaterial` (new or replacing existing ocean material):
```glsl
uniform float uTime;
uniform sampler2D uBathymetry; // depth data texture
uniform sampler2D uDensityMap; // vessel density (low-res, updated periodically)

varying vec2 vUv;

// Hexagonal grid SDF
float hexGrid(vec2 p, float scale) {
    p *= scale;
    vec2 h = vec2(1.0, sqrt(3.0));
    vec2 a = mod(p, h) - h * 0.5;
    vec2 b = mod(p + h * 0.5, h) - h * 0.5;
    vec2 g = length(a) < length(b) ? a : b;
    float d = max(abs(g.x), abs(g.y * 0.577 + abs(g.x) * 0.5));
    return smoothstep(0.02, 0.0, abs(d - 0.5));
}

void main() {
    float depth = texture2D(uBathymetry, vUv).r;
    
    // Depth-responsive base luminance
    float depthGlow = exp(-depth * 4.0) * 0.15; // shallower = brighter
    vec3 deepColor = vec3(0.0, 0.06, 0.12);
    vec3 shallowColor = vec3(0.0, 0.18, 0.22);
    vec3 oceanBase = mix(deepColor, shallowColor, depthGlow);
    
    // Animated grid
    vec2 distortedUv = vUv + vec2(
        sin(vUv.y * 8.0 + uTime * 0.15) * 0.003,
        cos(vUv.x * 6.0 + uTime * 0.12) * 0.003
    );
    float grid = hexGrid(distortedUv, 80.0);
    float density = texture2D(uDensityMap, vUv).r;
    float gridIntensity = 0.04 + density * 0.06; // brighter near vessels
    
    vec3 gridColor = vec3(0.1, 0.5, 0.6) * grid * gridIntensity;
    
    gl_FragColor = vec4(oceanBase + gridColor, 1.0);
}
```
- The density map can be generated CPU-side as a low-resolution (256×128) texture updated every few seconds, with vessel positions rasterized as gaussian splats. Alternatively, defer density-responsiveness and ship the static animated grid first.
- The depth luminance can sample from existing bathymetric data (the same data driving the contour lines at Hormuz — it's already in the pipeline).

**Affected Files:**
- `src/scene/OceanMaterial.js` — **NEW** or major modification of existing ocean/water surface material. Owns the shader, bathymetry texture binding, density map updates.
- `src/shaders/ocean.vert.glsl` / `src/shaders/ocean.frag.glsl` — **NEW**.
- `src/data/VesselDensityGenerator.js` — **NEW** (deferrable) — generates the low-res density texture from vessel positions.
- `src/scene/SceneSetup.js` — swap the ocean plane/sphere material to the new shader material.

**Complexity:** MEDIUM — The shader is ~50 lines and well within standard GLSL territory. The hexagonal grid SDF is a documented pattern. The depth texture binding depends on what format bathymetry data currently exists in. The density-responsive modulation adds a separate concern that can be deferred. A static animated grid is a one-day task; the full depth-responsive system is a one-week task.

**Holographic Vision:** The animated grid is arguably the single strongest "this is a holographic projection" signifier per pixel of screen area. Every canonical sci-fi tactical display — from *Ender's Game* to *The Expanse* to real-world CIC concepts — features structured void. It tells the viewer subliminally: "you are looking at a synthetic display generated by a machine, and that machine is watching everything." The depth-responsive luminance transforms the ocean from dead space into an intelligent data surface, and the density-responsive grid intensity creates the sense that the system is allocating rendering resources where attention is needed — which is exactly what a real tactical AI would do.

### Proposal 5: Terrain Rim Lighting and Coastline Edge Emission

**Impact:** Landmasses transform from dark silhouettes (Hormuz view) and flat texture decals (global/oblique views) into three-dimensional surfaces with legible topography. A low-angle directional light rakes across terrain, catching ridgelines and coastal cliffs with a faint cyan-white rim. The coastline itself — the land-sea boundary — gains a thin, bright emissive line: a glowing seam where two data domains meet. Elevated terrain shows subtle half-lambert shading — not photorealistic, but diagrammatic, like a military relief map rendered in light. At the oblique dawn angle, the sun-facing highlands pick up warm golden tones while shadow-side valleys deepen into cool indigo, with faint emissive dots (city/infrastructure data, if available) becoming visible in the darkness. The overall effect: terrain becomes a *surface that exists in a lighting environment* rather than a painted backdrop.

**Technique:**
- Add a `THREE.DirectionalLight` (intensity 0.15–0.25, cool blue-white `#8ec8e8`) positioned at ~15° elevation from the primary camera angle. Add a `THREE.HemisphereLight` (sky: `#0a2a3a`, ground: `#000000`, intensity: 0.08) for ambient fill that prevents pure-black shadows.
- Modify terrain `ShaderMaterial` (or create a new one) with:
```glsl
uniform vec3 uLightDir;
uniform vec3 uViewDir;

varying vec3 vNormal;
varying vec3 vWorldPos;

void main() {
    // Half-lambert directional shading
    float NdotL = dot(vNormal, uLightDir) * 0.5 + 0.5;
    NdotL = pow(NdotL, 1.5); // slightly sharpen the transition
    
    // Fresnel rim
    float rim = 1.0 - max(dot(normalize(uViewDir), vNormal), 0.0);
    rim = pow(rim, 3.5);
    vec3 rimColor = vec3(0.3, 0.75, 1.0) * rim * 0.45;
    
    vec3 baseColor = texture2D(uTerrainTexture, vUv).rgb;
    vec3 lit = baseColor * (0.3 + NdotL * 0.7); // ambient floor of 0.3
    
    gl_FragColor = vec4(lit + rimColor, 1.0);
}
```
- Coastline emission: render coastline polylines as a separate `THREE.Line` (or `THREE.LineSegments`) geometry with `LineBasicMaterial` using a bright cyan emissive color. Alternatively, detect the land-sea boundary in the terrain shader using a land mask and `fwidth()` for edge detection, and add emissive contribution at the boundary.
- **Prerequisite:** Terrain normals must be correct. If the terrain mesh doesn't have per-vertex normals (or they're all pointing up), compute them from the heightmap: either bake a normal map texture or compute in the vertex shader using finite differences on adjacent height samples.

**Affected Files:**
- `src/scene/TerrainMaterial.js` — **NEW** or major refactor of existing terrain/land surface material.
- `src/shaders/terrain.vert.glsl` / `src/shaders/terrain.frag.glsl` — **NEW**.
- `src/scene/SceneSetup.js` — add directional and hemisphere lights to the scene; swap terrain material.
- `src/scene/CoastlineRenderer.js` — **NEW** (if using separate line geometry for coastline glow) — loads coastline GeoJSON, generates `LineSegments` geometry, applies emissive material.

**Complexity:** MEDIUM-HIGH — The shader math is simple, but the prerequisite of correct terrain normals is the gating factor. If the terrain geometry is a flat plane with a displacement map, normals can be computed from the heightmap. If it's a pre-built mesh without normals, they need to be generated. The coastline emission (separate geometry approach) requires coastline boundary data, which may or may not exist in the current data pipeline. The directional light addition itself is trivial, but balancing it so data layers remain readable across all zoom levels requires iteration.

**Holographic Vision:** Terrain is the context layer — the physical world that all intelligence data is referenced against. When terrain is flat and unlit, the data floats on nothing. When terrain responds to light, the data floats *above something real*. The rim lighting specifically ties terrain into the same luminous visual language as the atmospheric shell (Proposal 1) and the vessel halos (Proposal 3), creating a unified world where everything is made of the same holographic light. The coastline emission transforms the most important geographic boundary (land/sea) into a glowing data seam — which is exactly what it is in the intelligence context.

### Proposal B: Vessel Point Emission with Animated Pulse Halos

**What it would look like:**
Each vessel dot becomes a **point of living light** — a bright core (white-hot center, colored by vessel type) surrounded by a soft radial falloff halo that breathes slowly (sinusoidal opacity oscillation, ~0.5 Hz, phase-randomized per vessel so they don't pulse in unison). Dark vessels get a distinctly different treatment: a slow, ominous red-orange pulse with a wider, dimmer halo — they should feel like warning beacons. When a vessel hasn't reported in a while, its halo contracts and dims. Fresh AIS pings produce a brief expanding ring — a sonar-ping ripple that fades over 2 seconds.

Right now the dots are **dead.** They communicate nothing about state, urgency, or recency. They're the same brightness as the landmass labels, which means they compete visually with text rather than dominating it as primary data.

**Technical approach:**
- Replace current vessel markers with `Points` using a custom `ShaderMaterial` and `gl_PointSize` scaled by zoom level.
- Fragment shader: radial SDF circle with **exponential falloff** for the halo: `exp(-dist * dist * sharpness)`. Core color from a `uniform`/`attribute` per vessel type.
- Pulse: `uniform float uTime` driving `sin(uTime * rate + phaseOffset) * 0.5 + 0.5` multiplied into alpha.
- Sonar ring: a per-vessel `float` attribute for `timeSinceLastPing`, driving a ring SDF (`abs(dist - expandRadius)`) that expands and fades. Can be batched in a single draw call using instanced attributes.
- The `UnrealBloomPass` from Proposal A will automatically pick up these emissive points and give them a natural glow — this is why A and B compound beautifully.

**Complexity:** MEDIUM — the shader math is straightforward. The main work is plumbing per-vessel attributes (type, phase, ping time) into the buffer geometry. If vessels are currently individual meshes, refactoring to instanced `Points` is the prerequisite.

**Why it matters:** Vessels are the primary data object. If they don't feel alive, nothing does. Pulsing, type-differentiated, bloom-caught light points are the difference between a database table rendered on a map and a living sensor display. This is the "breathing ecosystem" in the brief.

## Three Concrete Proposals

### 1. Atmospheric Rim + Horizon Haze Shell

**What it would look like:** A luminous, gossamer-thin shell around the globe's edge — thickening and intensifying at the horizon from this oblique angle. At dawn angle, the left/lower horizon glows warm amber-gold transitioning through salmon into pale blue, while the upper limb fades into a cold indigo-to-black gradient. The atmosphere should be visible as a distinct 2-3px bright line at the earth's edge (like ISS photography), with a soft falloff bloom extending maybe 30-50px into "space." Terrain near the horizon should progressively desaturate and blue-shift, as if seen through 200km of atmosphere. The overall effect: you instantly understand you're looking at a sphere with gas wrapped around it, catching dawn light.

**Three.js/GLSL technique:** Implement a slightly-oversized sphere mesh (atmosphere shell) with a custom `ShaderMaterial` using **Fresnel-based rim lighting**. The fragment shader calculates `dot(viewDirection, normal)` — where the view angle is grazing (near horizon), opacity and luminosity increase. Add a `uniform vec3 sunDirection` to shift the rim color warm on the sun-facing side. The haze effect on terrain uses a separate pass or modifies the terrain shader to blend toward a `fogColor` based on fragment distance from camera and angle to horizon. Classic approach: Sean O'Neil's atmospheric scattering adapted for stylized/holographic aesthetic, or simplified with a two-color Fresnel ramp.

```glsl
float rim = 1.0 - max(dot(viewDir, normal), 0.0);
float atmosphere = pow(rim, 3.0) * atmosphereIntensity;
vec3 atmColor = mix(coldColor, warmColor, dot(normal, sunDir) * 0.5 + 0.5);
gl_FragColor = vec4(atmColor, atmosphere * smoothstep(0.0, 0.3, rim));
```

**Complexity:** MEDIUM — The shell mesh is trivial, the shader is well-documented territory, but tuning the visual balance with the existing terrain colors and getting the dawn gradient to feel cinematic rather than cheap requires iteration. Integration with the existing render pipeline (making sure it composites correctly with bloom if present) adds some work.

**Why it matters for the vision:** This is THE fundamental separator between "a map" and "a planet." Every sci-fi command centre globe has atmospheric glow. It's what tells your brain "this is a living world" at a preverbal level. Without it, you have a data visualization. With it, you have an environment.

### 2. Volumetric God Rays from Dawn Position

**What it would look like:** From the dawn position (I'd place it just below the horizon at roughly the 7-8 o'clock position of the current view), shafts of warm golden light rake across the Atlantic Ocean surface. These aren't photorealistic — they should feel slightly digitized, like light passing through a holographic medium. Think: subtle radial streaks that are brighter where they cross ocean surface and slightly attenuated where they pass over landmass (suggesting the terrain has height that occludes). The rays should have a slight cyan-edged diffraction quality at their boundaries, tying them to the holographic UI palette. The ocean surface in the path of these rays should show subtle specular brightening — not full PBR water reflection, but a luminous sheen that says "this surface is receiving directional light."

**Three.js/GLSL technique:** Screen-space god rays via **radial blur post-processing**. Render bright emissive sources (a sun disc just at/below horizon, atmospheric rim highlights) to an occlusion buffer. Everything else renders black. Then apply a radial blur centered on the sun's screen-space position — iteratively sampling along the ray direction with falloff. This is the classic Crepuscular Rays technique (GPU Gems 3, Chapter 13). Implement as a custom `ShaderPass` in the post-processing pipeline:

```glsl
vec2 toSun = sunScreenPos - uv;
float totalWeight = 1.0;
vec4 color = texture2D(occlusionTex, uv);
for(int i = 1; i < NUM_SAMPLES; i++) {
    float t = float(i) / float(NUM_SAMPLES);
    vec2 samplePos = uv + toSun * t * density;
    color += texture2D(occlusionTex, samplePos) * weight;
    totalWeight += weight;
    weight *= decay;
}
```

For the holographic diffraction edge, add a subtle chromatic aberration offset on the ray samples — shift R and B channels slightly along the ray direction.

**Complexity:** MEDIUM-HIGH — The god ray pass itself is straightforward and well-documented. The challenge is: (a) generating a proper occlusion texture from the existing scene (terrain needs to correctly block), (b) the sun position needs to track the conceptual "time of day" system, (c) balancing intensity so it enhances without overwhelming the data layer. The chromatic edge detail is a nice-to-have flourish that adds LOW additional complexity once the base pass exists.

**Why it matters for the vision:** God rays are the single most evocative atmospheric lighting effect. They create the feeling of *witnessing a moment in time* — that this isn't a static visualization but a living system with a sun, with weather, with a diurnal cycle. For a tactical map, this subtly communicates temporal awareness. And the holographic diffraction treatment keeps it from feeling like a nature documentary and places it firmly in the sci-fi command aesthetic.

### 3. Terrain-Interactive Emissive Lighting Layer

**What it would look like:** The landmass terrain should respond to the dawn light direction with a physically-motivated but stylistically exaggerated shading model. Facing the dawn: warm golden highlights on mountain ridges and elevated terrain. Facing away: deep cool shadows in valleys, with city/infrastructure points (if data exists) glowing as faint amber-orange emissive dots that become more visible in the shadowed regions. The ocean surface gets a subtle animated caustic pattern in the shallows near coastlines where dawn light hits — very faint, almost subliminal, but adding life. The bathymetric shelves (which already have good color data) get a slight luminous edge highlight where they drop off into deep water, as if the depth contour itself is a glowing scan line.

On the continent, the current flat color-mapped terrain should gain a simple **half-lambert directional shading** — not full PBR (too realistic, wrong aesthetic), but enough to reveal surface topology through light and shadow. The Ethiopian Highlands, the Great Rift Valley, the Atlas Mountains — these should cast computational shadows that make the terrain feel three-dimensional even from this distance.

**Three.js/GLSL technique:** Modify the terrain surface shader to include:
- **Directional lighting with half-lambert wrap:** `float NdotL = dot(normal, sunDir) * 0.5 + 0.5` — this prevents pure-black shadows while still creating clear light/dark differentiation
- **Emissive data overlay:** Multiply a data texture (city lights, infrastructure) by `(1.0 - NdotL)` so it reveals in shadow
- **Bathymetric edge detection:** Use `fwidth()` on depth values to find steep gradients and add a cyan emissive line
- **Shallow water caustics:** Simple animated voronoi or wave-interference pattern in the fragment shader, masked to shallow depth regions, very low opacity (0.05-0.1)

The normals for the terrain can be computed from the heightmap in the vertex shader or baked into a normal map texture.

**Complexity:** HIGH — This touches the core terrain rendering pipeline. Computing or supplying terrain normals is the fundamental prerequisite. The shading model itself is simple, but integrating it without breaking the existing color scheme, ensuring performance with the heightmap-derived normals, and tuning all the emissive layers to coexist requires significant iteration. The caustics alone are a small sub-project.

**Why it matters for the vision:** This is what transforms the map surface from a *texture* into a *world*. Right now the terrain is essentially a flat image projected onto geometry. With light-responsive shading, the terrain becomes a surface that exists in a lighting environment — it has a relationship with the sun, with time, with the atmosphere. This is the physical grounding that makes the holographic data layers (vessel tracks, sensor coverage, alert zones) feel like they're floating *above* something real rather than pasted onto a backdrop. The emissive city lights appearing in shadow is the kind of detail that makes an operator lean forward and think "this is alive."
